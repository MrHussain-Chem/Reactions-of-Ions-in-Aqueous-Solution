1. Hydrated Metal Cations

Metal ions in aqueous solution exist as hexa-aqua complex ions

💧Key definitions (AQA spec language)

▼
Ligand AQA Spec 3.2.5 A molecule or ion that forms a co-ordinate (dative covalent) bond to a metal ion by donating a lone pair of electrons to it.
Complex Ion AQA Spec 3.2.5 A metal ion surrounded by ligands bonded by co-ordinate (dative covalent) bonds.
Co-ordination Number AQA Spec 3.2.5 The total number of co-ordinate bonds formed between the central metal ion and its ligands.
⚠️ AQA Terminology Alert AQA mark schemes require you to use the terms co-ordinate bond or dative covalent bond when describing ligand bonding. Using "covalent bond" alone will not score the mark. The ligand donates the lone pair — the metal ion accepts it.

When metal salts dissolve in water, the metal cation is surrounded by six water molecules as ligands, each donating a lone pair to the metal ion via a co-ordinate bond. This gives an octahedral complex ion with a co-ordination number of 6.

🎨Colours of hexa-aqua ions in solution

▼
Metal ionFormula of hexa-aqua ionColour in solution
Fe2+[Fe(H2O)6]2+Pale green
Cu2+[Cu(H2O)6]2+Blue
Fe3+[Fe(H2O)6]3+Pale violet — appears yellow-brown in practice due to partial hydrolysis forming [Fe(H2O)5(OH)]2+
Al3+[Al(H2O)6]3+Colourless (Al3+ is not a transition metal ion — d sub-shell is empty)

Why are transition metal ions coloured? The five d orbitals in a transition metal complex are split into two energy levels. Electrons absorb specific wavelengths of visible light to be promoted from lower to higher d orbitals. The remaining (transmitted) wavelengths give the ion its observed colour. Al3+ and other ions with a full or empty d sub-shell cannot do this and so are colourless.

⚠️ AQA Mark Scheme: [Fe(H2O)6]3+ solutions appear yellow-brown in practice, not purple. AQA examiners expect students to state that the pale violet colour is due to the actual [Fe(H2O)6]3+ complex, but the yellow-brown appearance in solution is caused by partial hydrolysis producing [Fe(H2O)5(OH)]2+. Do not describe the solution as simply "purple."

2. Acidity of Hexa-aqua Ions

Why metal salt solutions are acidic — and how charge density explains the difference

🧪Hydrolysis and the production of H+

▼
Bronsted-Lowry Acid AQA Spec 3.1.2 A proton donor — a species that donates H+ ions to another species.
Hydrolysis AQA Spec 3.2.6 The breaking of a bond using water. In metal aqua ions, this refers to the loss of H+ from a co-ordinated water ligand.

The positive charge on the metal ion withdraws electron density from the O-H bonds in the co-ordinated water ligands, polarising those bonds. If the metal ion has sufficient charge density, the O-H bond weakens enough for a proton (H+) to be donated to a free water molecule, making the solution acidic.

Hydrolysis of a 3+ aqua ion — AQA accepted form: [M(H2O)6]3+(aq) + H2O(l) ⇌ [M(H2O)5(OH)]2+(aq) + H3O+(aq)
Hydrolysis of a 2+ aqua ion — AQA accepted form: [M(H2O)6]2+(aq) + H2O(l) ⇌ [M(H2O)5(OH)]+(aq) + H3O+(aq)
⚠️ AQA Equation Requirements These equations must use a reversible arrow (⇌) — hydrolysis is an equilibrium process. Using a one-way arrow will lose the mark. AQA also accepts H+(aq) in place of H3O+(aq), but H3O+ is preferred as it more accurately represents what happens.

📊Effect of charge density on acidity

▼
Aqua ionTypical pH of solution
[M(H2O)6]+~7 (neutral)
[M(H2O)6]2+~6 (weakly acidic)
[M(H2O)6]3+~3 (more acidic)
[M(H2O)6]4+~0 (strongly acidic)
✏️ AQA Mark Scheme — Chain of Reasoning for Acidity Questions To gain full marks, you must state ALL of the following steps in order:
  1. The metal ion has a higher charge and/or smaller ionic radius → higher charge density
  2. The metal ion polarises the O-H bond in the co-ordinated water ligand more strongly
  3. The O-H bond is weakened more
  4. A proton (H+) is more easily donated to a water molecule → lower pH
Stating only "higher charge" or "more acidic" without the full reasoning chain will not score all marks.
Illustrative pKa values — lower pKa = stronger acid (equilibrium lies further right): [Fe(H2O)6]2+  pKa ≈ 5.9
[Fe(H2O)6]3+  pKa ≈ 2.5  ← lower pKa = stronger acid
[Al(H2O)6]3+  pKa ≈ 4.9
[Cu(H2O)6]2+  pKa ≈ 7.5
✅ Note: These pKa values are not required by the AQA specification — they are included here to illustrate relative acid strengths. You need to know the trend (higher charge density → lower pKa → stronger acid) but do not need to memorise specific values.

⚗️Worked Example — Comparing acidity of [Al(H2O)6]3+ and [Cu(H2O)6]2+

▼
⚗️ Predict and explain which ion produces the lower pH solution: [Al(H2O)6]3+ or [Cu(H2O)6]2+
1
State the prediction: [Al(H2O)6]3+ will produce the lower pH solution.
2
Charge density: Al3+ has a higher charge (+3) and a smaller ionic radius than Cu2+. This gives Al3+ a greater charge density.
3
Polarisation: The higher charge density of Al3+ causes greater polarisation of the O-H bonds in the co-ordinated water ligands.
4
Bond weakening: The O-H bonds in [Al(H2O)6]3+ are weakened to a greater extent than in [Cu(H2O)6]2+.
5
Conclusion: Protons (H+) are more easily donated to free water molecules from [Al(H2O)6]3+ → greater [H+] → lower pH.

3. Reactions with Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH)

OH- ions drive hydrolysis further — forming insoluble metal hydroxide precipitates

🔬Mechanism — what happens when NaOH is added?

▼

OH- ions from NaOH act as a Bronsted-Lowry base (proton acceptor), removing H+ ions from the co-ordinated water ligands. This shifts the hydrolysis equilibrium to the right (Le Chatelier's principle), producing a neutral, uncharged metal hydroxide complex that is insoluble in water and precipitates out of solution.

General equation — M3+ with NaOH (AQA accepted form): [M(H2O)6]3+(aq) + 3OH-(aq) ⇌ [M(H2O)3(OH)3](s) + 3H2O(l)
General equation — M2+ with NaOH (AQA accepted form): [M(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 2OH-(aq) ⇌ [M(H2O)4(OH)2](s) + 2H2O(l)
✏️ AQA Equation Requirements These equations use a reversible arrow (⇌) because the reaction can be reversed by adding acid. The precipitate is uncharged and insoluble — AQA mark schemes credit explanations that state "the neutral/uncharged complex is insoluble."

⚠️ AQA Mark Scheme: When describing the Fe2+ precipitate darkening, you must state it turns brown due to oxidation by oxygen in the air, converting Fe(II) hydroxide [Fe(H2O)4(OH)2] to Fe(III) hydroxide [Fe(H2O)3(OH)3]. Simply saying "it turns brown" alone does not score the explanation mark.

4. Reactions with Aqueous Ammonia (NH3)

Same initial precipitates as NaOH — but one key difference with Cu2+ in excess

💨Mechanism — what happens when NH3 is added?

▼

Aqueous ammonia is a weak base. It acts as a Bronsted-Lowry base (proton acceptor), removing H+ from the co-ordinated water ligands. This produces the same insoluble metal hydroxide precipitates as NaOH, but via formation of NH4+ rather than water.

General equation — M3+ with NH3: [M(H2O)6]3+(aq) + 3NH3(aq) ⇌ [M(H2O)3(OH)3](s) + 3NH4+(aq)
General equation — M2+ with NH3: [M(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 2NH3(aq) ⇌ [M(H2O)4(OH)2](s) + 2NH4+(aq)

🧫Specific reactions with NH3 — observations and equations

▼
Ion+ NH3 (dropwise)+ NH3 (excess)
[Fe(H2O)6]2+ Green precipitate [Fe(H2O)4(OH)2](s) No further reaction
[Cu(H2O)6]2+ Pale blue precipitate [Cu(H2O)4(OH)2](s) Pale blue precipitate dissolves to give a deep blue solution of [Cu(H2O)2(NH3)4]2+ — ligand substitution
[Fe(H2O)6]3+ Brown precipitate [Fe(H2O)3(OH)3](s) No further reaction
[Al(H2O)6]3+ White precipitate [Al(H2O)3(OH)3](s) No further reaction — NH3 is too weak a base to dissolve the precipitate
⚠️ AQA Colour Distinction — Cu2+ with NaOH vs NH3 The critical colour distinction AQA mark schemes test is between the precipitate stage and the deep blue solution formed with excess NH3. Most mark schemes accept "blue precipitate" for both the NaOH and dropwise NH3 stages (since both produce the same compound). However, the safest approach is:
  • Cu2+ + NaOH → blue precipitate
  • Cu2+ + NH3 (dropwise) → pale blue precipitate
  • Cu2+ + NH3 (excess) → deep blue solution ← this is the distinction that must be made

Key equations for Cu2+ + NH3:

Cu2+ + limited NH3 — hydrolysis (pale blue precipitate): [Cu(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 2NH3(aq) ⇌ [Cu(H2O)4(OH)2](s) + 2NH4+(aq)
Cu2+ + excess NH3 — ligand substitution (deep blue solution): [Cu(H2O)4(OH)2](s) + 4NH3(aq) ⇌ [Cu(H2O)2(NH3)4]2+(aq) + 2H2O(l) + 2OH-(aq)
Overall equation — Cu2+ + excess NH3 (AQA accepted combined form): [Cu(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 4NH3(aq) ⇌ [Cu(H2O)2(NH3)4]2+(aq) + 4H2O(l)
✅ AQA Key Distinction — NaOH vs NH3 with Al3+: With excess NaOH, the Al(OH)3 precipitate dissolves (amphoteric behaviour). With excess NH3, the precipitate does not dissolve. AQA examiners expect the reason: NH3 is a weaker base than NaOH and cannot provide a high enough concentration of OH- to further deprotonate Al(OH)3.
⚠️ AQA Mark Scheme: The formula for the product of Cu2+ + excess NH3 must be written as [Cu(H2O)2(NH3)4]2+ — only four of the six water ligands are substituted. Writing [Cu(NH3)4]2+ or [Cu(NH3)6]2+ will not score the formula mark.

⚗️Worked Example — Cu2+ + excess NH3 (two-stage answer)

▼
⚗️ Describe and explain what happens when NH3(aq) is added dropwise to [Cu(H2O)6]2+, then in excess.
1
Observation (dropwise): A pale blue precipitate forms.
2
Explanation: NH3 acts as a Bronsted-Lowry base, accepting H+ from the co-ordinated water ligands (hydrolysis). The neutral, uncharged [Cu(H2O)4(OH)2] is insoluble and precipitates.
3
Equation 1: [Cu(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 2NH3(aq) ⇌ [Cu(H2O)4(OH)2](s) + 2NH4+(aq)
4
Observation (excess NH3): The pale blue precipitate dissolves to give a deep blue solution.
5
Explanation: Ligand substitution occurs — four NH3 molecules replace four H2O ligands around the Cu2+ ion, forming [Cu(H2O)2(NH3)4]2+. NH3 and H2O are similar-sized ligands, so the co-ordination number remains 6.
6
Equation 2: [Cu(H2O)4(OH)2](s) + 4NH3(aq) ⇌ [Cu(H2O)2(NH3)4]2+(aq) + 2H2O(l) + 2OH-(aq)

5. Reactions with Sodium Carbonate (Na2CO3)

The most important distinction — M2+ vs M3+ with carbonate ions

🧻M2+ ions + Na2CO3 — simple precipitation

▼

M2+ aqua ions have a relatively low charge density and are only weakly acidic. They are not acidic enough to donate protons to CO3 2- ions. Instead, a simple ionic precipitation reaction occurs, forming insoluble metal carbonates. No gas is produced.

Cu2+ + Na2CO3 → blue-green precipitate: [Cu(H2O)6]2+(aq) + CO3 2-(aq) → CuCO3(s) + 6H2O(l)
Fe2+ + Na2CO3 → green precipitate: [Fe(H2O)6]2+(aq) + CO3 2-(aq) → FeCO3(s) + 6H2O(l)
✏️ AQA Equation Requirement These equations use a one-way arrow (→) because precipitation reactions go to completion. Note the product is the metal carbonate — CuCO3 and FeCO3 — not the hydroxide.

🌡️M3+ ions + Na2CO3 — hydrolysis and CO2 gas

▼

M3+ aqua ions have a high charge density and are sufficiently acidic to donate protons to CO3 2- ions. CO3 2- acts as a Bronsted-Lowry base, accepting H+ from the co-ordinated water ligands. This drives hydrolysis — the metal hydroxide precipitates and CO2 gas is evolved.

Fe3+ + Na2CO3 → brown precipitate + CO2 gas: 2[Fe(H2O)6]3+(aq) + 3CO3 2-(aq) → 2[Fe(H2O)3(OH)3](s) + 3CO2(g) + 3H2O(l)
Al3+ + Na2CO3 → white precipitate + CO2 gas: 2[Al(H2O)6]3+(aq) + 3CO3 2-(aq) → 2[Al(H2O)3(OH)3](s) + 3CO2(g) + 3H2O(l)
✅ AQA Key Rule:
  • M2+ + CO3 2- → metal carbonate precipitate, no gas
  • M3+ + CO3 2- → metal hydroxide precipitate + CO2 gas
The difference arises because [M(H2O)6]3+ is acidic enough to protonate CO3 2-, but [M(H2O)6]2+ is not.
⚠️ AQA Mark Scheme — Common Error: Writing Fe2(CO3)3, Al2(CO3)3, or any carbonate salt as the product of M3+ + Na2CO3 will score zero. The product is always the metal hydroxide when a 3+ ion reacts with Na2CO3 — these carbonate salts do not exist under aqueous conditions.

⚗️Worked Example — Explaining the M2+ vs M3+ difference

▼
⚗️ Explain why Fe3+(aq) produces different products to Fe2+(aq) when Na2CO3(aq) is added. [4 marks]
1
Fe3+ has a higher charge and smaller ionic radius than Fe2+, giving it a greater charge density.
2
This means [Fe(H2O)6]3+ is a stronger Bronsted-Lowry acid (better proton donor) than [Fe(H2O)6]2+.
3
[Fe(H2O)6]3+ is acidic enough to donate H+ to CO3 2- ions. CO3 2- acts as a base, accepting H+ → CO2 + H2O. The metal undergoes hydrolysis → brown precipitate of [Fe(H2O)3(OH)3] + CO2(g).
4
[Fe(H2O)6]2+ is not acidic enough to protonate CO3 2-. Simple ionic precipitation occurs instead → green precipitate of FeCO3(s), no gas evolved.

6. Reactions with Conc. HCl & Acid/Base Character of Hydroxides

Ligand substitution with Cl- and the amphoteric nature of Al(OH)3

🟡Adding concentrated HCl — ligand substitution

▼

Concentrated HCl provides a high concentration of Cl- ions. Cl- is a larger ligand than H2O, so only four can fit around the central metal ion. This results in a change in co-ordination number (6 → 4) and shape (octahedral → tetrahedral). The reaction is a ligand substitution.

Aqua ionProduct with excess Cl-Colour change
[Fe(H2O)6]2+[FeCl4]2-Pale green → Yellow
[Cu(H2O)6]2+[CuCl4]2-Blue → Yellow-green
[Fe(H2O)6]3+[FeCl4]-Yellow-brown → Yellow
[Al(H2O)6]3+[AlCl4]-Colourless → Colourless
General AQA equation (e.g. Cu2+): [Cu(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 4Cl-(aq) ⇌ [CuCl4]2-(aq) + 6H2O(l)
✏️ AQA Charge Calculation — Very Frequently Penalised You must recalculate the charge on the chloro complex. Example for Cu2+:
Cu is +2; four Cl- each contribute -1 (total -4); overall charge = 2 + (-4) = -2
Product is [CuCl4]2- not [CuCl4]2+. Likewise [FeCl4]2- for Fe2+ and [FeCl4]- for Fe3+.

🔑AQA key patterns and rules

▼

NaOH vs NH3

  • Both produce same ppts initially (hydrolysis mechanism)
  • Excess NaOH: only Al(OH)3 dissolves (amphoteric)
  • Excess NH3: only Cu(OH)2 dissolves (ligand substitution to deep blue)
  • NH3 is too weak a base to dissolve Al(OH)3

Na2CO3 rule

  • M2+ → carbonate ppt, no gas (simple precipitation)
  • M3+ → hydroxide ppt + CO2 gas (hydrolysis)
  • Reason: M3+ has higher charge density → more acidic → can protonate CO3 2-

Conc. HCl

  • All ions: ligand substitution, H2O replaced by Cl-
  • Co-ordination number 6 → 4 (Cl- is larger than H2O)
  • Shape: octahedral → tetrahedral
  • Recalculate charge on product every time

Amphoteric Al(OH)3

  • Only Al(OH)3 dissolves in excess NaOH → [Al(H2O)2(OH)4]-
  • Does NOT dissolve in excess NH3
  • Dissolves in acid → [Al(H2O)6]3+
  • Fe, Cu hydroxides are basic only — never amphoteric

🔬 Required Practical 11

Carry out simple test-tube reactions to identify transition metal ions in aqueous solution

📋 AQA Specification Statement (3.2.6) Carry out simple test-tube reactions to identify the following metal ions in aqueous solution: Fe2+, Cu2+, Fe3+ and Al3+, using the bases NaOH(aq), NH3(aq) and Na2CO3(aq). This is examined in context in Paper 1 and may appear as a practical skills question in Paper 3.

🎯Aim and overview

▼

The aim is to use the characteristic colours and behaviours of precipitates formed with different reagents to identify unknown metal ion solutions. The ions being identified are: Fe2+, Cu2+, Fe3+ and Al3+.

The three reagents used are:

  • NaOH(aq) — added dropwise, then in excess
  • NH3(aq) — added dropwise, then in excess
  • Na2CO3(aq) — added; observe whether gas is produced

⚠️Hazards and risk assessment

▼
🧪 Hazards — required for exam questions on this practical:
  • NaOH(aq) — corrosive (concentrated) / irritant (dilute). Wear safety goggles and gloves.
  • NH3(aq) — irritant, strong pungent smell; vapour is toxic in high concentrations. Use in a well-ventilated laboratory or fume cupboard.
  • Na2CO3(aq) — low hazard at dilute concentrations, irritant if concentrated. Wear goggles.
  • Metal salt solutions (e.g. FeSO4, CuSO4, FeCl3, AlCl3) — irritants; some transition metal salts are harmful. Avoid skin contact.

📝Method — step by step

▼
1
Label 12 test tubes in a rack — four rows (one per ion) and three columns (one per reagent).
2
Using a clean pipette, add approximately 2 cm3 of each metal ion solution to the appropriately labelled test tubes. Record the initial colour.
3
NaOH test: Add NaOH(aq) dropwise. Record the colour of any precipitate. Continue adding NaOH until clearly in excess and record any further changes (particularly for Al3+).
4
NH3 test: Repeat using NH3(aq). Note the precise shade of the precipitate and observe whether it dissolves in excess (Cu2+ only).
5
Na2CO3 test: Add Na2CO3(aq). Observe precipitate colour and note whether bubbles of gas (CO2) are produced — this is the key M2+ vs M3+ distinction.
6
For the Fe2+ test tubes, leave to stand in air for several minutes. Record any colour change (green ppt darkens to brown).
7
Record all results systematically using precise AQA colour terminology.
✅ AQA Practical Tip: Always add reagents dropwise at first. Adding too much at once masks intermediate observations.

📖Practical exam questions with mark schemes

▼

Practical Q1 — Identifying an unknown ion

4 marks

A student tests an unknown solution. A white precipitate forms on addition of NaOH(aq). The precipitate dissolves to give a colourless solution when NaOH is added in excess. When NH3(aq) is added in excess to a separate sample, the white precipitate remains.

(a) Identify the metal ion present. [1]
(b) Write an equation for the reaction with excess NaOH. [2]
(c) Explain why the precipitate dissolves in excess NaOH but not in excess NH3. [1]

✓(a) Al3+ / [Al(H2O)6]3+
✓(b) [Al(H2O)3(OH)3](s) + OH-(aq) → [Al(H2O)2(OH)4]-(aq) + H2O(l)
✓(b) Correct state symbols throughout
✓(c) NH3 is a weaker base than NaOH / cannot provide a sufficiently high [OH-] to further deprotonate Al(OH)3
⚠️ Note: "NH3 doesn't react" scores zero — it does react initially to form the ppt. The mark is for explaining NH3 is too weak a base to dissolve the precipitate.

Practical Q2 — Distinguishing Fe2+ from Fe3+

5 marks

A student has two solutions, X and Y. Both contain iron salts. Describe how to determine which contains Fe2+ and which contains Fe3+. Include observations and equations. [5]

✓Observe colour — Fe2+ is pale green; Fe3+ is yellow-brown
✓Add NaOH(aq) dropwise — Fe2+ gives green ppt; Fe3+ gives brown ppt
✓Fe2+: [Fe(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 2OH-(aq) ⇌ [Fe(H2O)4(OH)2](s) + 2H2O(l)
✓Fe3+: [Fe(H2O)6]3+(aq) + 3OH-(aq) ⇌ [Fe(H2O)3(OH)3](s) + 3H2O(l)
✓Further: Na2CO3 — Fe3+ gives brown ppt + CO2; Fe2+ gives green ppt, no gas. OR: Fe2+ ppt turns brown in air (oxidation)

Q1 — Hydrolysis and acidity

5 marks

A student dissolves iron(III) chloride in water. The resulting solution has a pH of approximately 3.

(a) Write an equation to show why the solution is acidic. Include state symbols. [2]
(b) Explain why a solution of iron(II) chloride of the same concentration would have a higher pH. [3]

✓(a) [Fe(H2O)6]3+(aq) + H2O(l) ⇌ [Fe(H2O)5(OH)]2+(aq) + H3O+(aq) — reversible arrow required
✓(a) Correct state symbols throughout
✓(b) Fe3+ has a higher charge density (higher charge and/or smaller radius) than Fe2+
✓(b) Fe3+ polarises the O-H bonds in the co-ordinated water ligands to a greater extent, weakening them more
✓(b) Protons are more easily released from [Fe(H2O)6]3+ → greater [H+] → lower pH
⚠️ Common error: Stating only "Fe3+ has a higher charge" without linking to O-H bond polarisation and H+ release. All three steps required.
✅ Note: Using → instead of ⇌ in equation (a) loses 1 mark.

Q2 — Reactions with NaOH

6 marks

Describe what you would observe, and write equations, when NaOH is added dropwise and then in excess to a solution of aluminium sulfate. [6]

✓A white precipitate forms
✓[Al(H2O)6]3+(aq) + 3OH-(aq) ⇌ [Al(H2O)3(OH)3](s) + 3H2O(l)
✓On addition of excess NaOH, the white precipitate dissolves
✓A colourless solution is formed
✓[Al(H2O)3(OH)3](s) + OH-(aq) → [Al(H2O)2(OH)4]-(aq) + H2O(l)
✓State symbols correct throughout
✅ Language: Write "colourless solution" not "clear solution." Clear = transparent; colourless = no colour.

Q3 — Na2CO3 with M2+ and M3+ ions

5 marks

When Na2CO3 is added to iron(III) sulfate, a brown precipitate and gas are produced. When added to iron(II) sulfate, only a green precipitate forms — no gas.

Explain this difference and include equations. [5]

✓[Fe(H2O)6]3+ has higher charge density — higher charge, smaller radius
✓[Fe(H2O)6]3+ is sufficiently acidic to donate H+ to CO3 2-; [Fe(H2O)6]2+ is not
✓CO3 2- acts as a base with Fe3+ → hydrolysis → brown ppt + CO2
✓Fe3+: 2[Fe(H2O)6]3+(aq) + 3CO3 2-(aq) → 2[Fe(H2O)3(OH)3](s) + 3CO2(g) + 3H2O(l)
✓Fe2+: [Fe(H2O)6]2+(aq) + CO3 2-(aq) → FeCO3(s) + 6H2O(l)
⚠️ Zero marks error: Writing Fe2(CO3)3 as a product. This compound does not exist under aqueous conditions.

Q4 — Cu2+ with excess ammonia

5 marks

Describe what you would observe when NH3(aq) is added dropwise and then in excess to copper(II) sulfate solution. Write equations for each stage and identify the type of reaction in the final step. [5]

✓A pale blue precipitate forms
✓[Cu(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 2NH3(aq) ⇌ [Cu(H2O)4(OH)2](s) + 2NH4+(aq)
✓The pale blue precipitate dissolves to give a deep blue solution
✓[Cu(H2O)4(OH)2](s) + 4NH3(aq) ⇌ [Cu(H2O)2(NH3)4]2+(aq) + 2H2O(l) + 2OH-(aq)
✓Ligand substitution — four NH3 replace four H2O around Cu2+
⚠️ Common error: Writing [Cu(NH3)4]2+ or [Cu(NH3)6]2+. Correct formula is [Cu(H2O)2(NH3)4]2+.

Q5 — Concentrated HCl and ligand substitution

4 marks

When conc. HCl is added to [Cu(H2O)6]2+, the solution turns yellow-green.

(a) Write an equation. Include state symbols. [2]
(b) State the change in co-ordination number and shape, and explain why. [2]

✓(a) [Cu(H2O)6]2+(aq) + 4Cl-(aq) ⇌ [CuCl4]2-(aq) + 6H2O(l)
✓(a) Correct state symbols
✓(b) Co-ordination number 6 → 4; octahedral → tetrahedral
✓(b) Cl- is larger than H2O — only four Cl- fit around Cu2+
⚠️ Common error: Writing [CuCl4]2+ instead of [CuCl4]2-. Recalculate: Cu(+2) + 4Cl(-1 each) = -2 overall.

Q6 — Multi-step synoptic identification

6 marks

Solution P: White ppt with NaOH, dissolves in excess → colourless soln. White ppt with Na2CO3 + CO2 gas.
Solution Q: Blue ppt with NaOH, no change in excess. Blue-green ppt with Na2CO3, no gas.

(a) Identify P and Q. [2]
(b) Write equation for P + excess NaOH. [2]
(c) Explain why P produces CO2 but Q does not. [2]

✓(a) P = Al3+; Q = Cu2+
✓(b) [Al(H2O)3(OH)3](s) + OH-(aq) → [Al(H2O)2(OH)4]-(aq) + H2O(l)
✓(b) Correct state symbols
✓(c) Al3+ has higher charge density → stronger Bronsted-Lowry acid than Cu2+
✓(c) Al3+ is acidic enough to donate H+ to CO3 2- → CO2; Cu2+ is not → simple precipitation of CuCO3
✅ Exam technique: Work systematically — use each observation to eliminate possibilities, then explain using charge density and acid strength.
